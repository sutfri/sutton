SUTTON FRITZ
CRIMES OF THE FUTURE ETHICS REVIEW
Crimes of the Future (2022), directed by David Cronenberg, is a thought provoking sci-fi thriller set in the near future. In this future, people have lost the ability to feel pain and can no longer contract infections; consequently, self-mutilation becomes an erotic activity due to the insatiable hunger for sensation. There are two major plot points in this film that pertain to biomedical ethics: is performing surgery for pleasure doing “no harm”, and can a cadaver consent to dissection?

“Surgery is the new sex,” whispered Timlin fantasizing the sensation of scalpels after watching Caprice sensually lacerate Tenser. Before sensations went dull, Caprice was a trauma surgeon, but now she performs pornographic surgeries on her partner, Tenser, as performance art. For the sake of pleasure, does Caprice fail to practice non-maleficence? In this future, “performance art” is a line of work for doctors where they mutilate their patient in front of an audience. The ethical dilemma arises when you consider that there is no pain and no risk of infection, so are these pleasurable operations doing “no harm”? I argue yes, doctors are not causing harm and are keeping to non-maleficence because there is very little possibility for fatality. To support my philosophy with an analogy, current cosmetic surgery is inherently risky– as is everything, ever–, but plastic surgeons still harm patients by cutting into them despite the surgery almost always being unnecessary to begin with.

Moreover, at what point does emotional gratification outway physical conservation? If this threshold is surpassed, beneficence can contradict non-maleficence. Returning to the analogy of current cosmetic surgery, reconstructing a body feature may pose greater mental and emotional benefits than the drawbacks of the physical recovery process. In this sense, the pleasure of mutilation in a world of monotony almost certainly outways the physical risks of the operations.

The latter half of the film debates whether it is ethical to dissect the body of a young boy, named Brecken, as performance art. Despite receiving consent from both of the boy’s parents, a public display of his dissection does not keep to his autonomy by breaking his anonymity. In fact, this “performance” was named the “Brecken Show”.

Dr.Caprice took issue with dead Brecken being incapable of consenting to the show, so her performance partner, Tenser, dissected him instead. Therefore, despite being severely unqualified to perform the autopsy, Tenser did not break any of the four principles of biomedical ethics since he was never bound by them in the first place as he is not a medical professional. Although Dr.Caprice did not perform the dissection she was present and commentating on the operation; therefore, she is guilty of not respecting the boy’s autonomy. Her caution regarding consent is valid, however, Caprice’s mode of arrival for her personal morals are in vain due to her need to inspire an emotional response to her art.

In all, Crimes of the Future discussed many complex themes regarding art, technology, evolution, and biomedical ethics. The latter topic was not overlooked by the film’s director, Cronenberg, as I have argued a couple very compelling instances regarding the ethics of his fictional future and its possible crimes.
